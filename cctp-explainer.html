How ChainMore moves USDC across chains

A short technical 1-pager for engineers and finance teams evaluating ChainMore against Bridge.xyz, BVNK, and similar stablecoin-payment providers.

The headline: CCTP is ChainMore's first Mini-MVP rail, not the whole ChainMore product. In this path, the customer signs the source-chain burn directly, Circle burns native USDC on the source chain, and Circle mints native USDC on the destination chain. ChainMore's destination contract then validates the source burn and applies the accepted settlement pattern before merchant forwarding. Final settlement timing depends on that pattern, which is currently in architecture review.

The flow, in 5 stages

1
Customer signs depositForBurn on the source chain.

The customer's wallet (MetaMask, Coinbase Wallet, Rainbow, any WalletConnect-compatible) signs a single transaction calling Circle's TokenMessengerV2 contract. ChainMore never holds the signing key.

2
Source chain burns the USDC and emits a Message.

USDC is destroyed on the source chain. CCTP's MessageSent event captures the burn, including the destination chain, amount, and recipient. ChainMore's backend monitors for the event but does not custody the funds.

3
Circle's attestor service signs the burn proof.

Circle's offchain attestor verifies the burn happened and signs the message. Our backend polls Circle's iris-api for the signed attestation. With CCTP V2 Fast Transfer, this typically takes 30 seconds.

4
The signed proof is submitted on the destination chain.

The proof is submitted to Circle's MessageTransmitterV2 directly or through ChainMore's destination settlement contract, depending on the accepted settlement pattern. CCTP's message binds the destination recipient; ChainMore's relayer role, where used, is gas submission and status orchestration, not discretionary custody.

5
Destination settlement completes under audited contract rules.

Before mainnet launch, ChainMore will use an audited settlement pattern that validates the source burn, enforces replay protection, applies the configured fee, forwards net USDC to the merchant, and preserves reconciliation. Circle's core CCTP contracts do not execute an automatic recipient hook; ChainMore's settlement logic is integrator-owned and documented in the replacement ADR.

What this gives the merchant

  • Fast native-USDC movement: CCTP V2 Fast Transfer targets fast attestation where supported; ChainMore's end-to-end settlement SLO will be revalidated with the replacement settlement pattern.
  • No third-party bridge or LP: no liquidity-provider slippage, no wrapped-asset bridge layer. Circle's protocol burns USDC on one chain and mints native USDC on the other; the supply is unchanged. Smart-contract risk and protocol risk remain. See Trust model and audits below.
  • Native USDC at destination: not a wrapped or bridged variant. The merchant receives the same Circle-issued USDC they would receive on a same-chain payment.
  • Sub-30bps fee: ChainMore takes 15 bps for the orchestration. CCTP V2 Fast Transfer adds a small variable fee paid through to Circle.
  • Idempotency by design: the protocol's nonce-binding makes the destination mint replay-safe at the contract level. Multiple submissions of the same proof produce one mint.

Compare to a custodial provider

Custodial provider (e.g. Bridge.xyz)

Customer pays into provider's wallet. Provider holds funds, runs its own bridge / swap / KYB layer, eventually pays merchant. Provider is regulated as a CASP/MSB; merchants undergo full KYB before onboarding. 6+ weeks of contract negotiation common.

ChainMore (orchestrator)

Customer signs transaction directly. Funds flow through Circle's permissionless protocol; ChainMore never custodies. Light merchant-onboarding (email, wallet address, sanctions screening). Days, not weeks. We position ourselves as a software / IaaS provider; our regulatory classification is being validated by Estonian counsel and we'll publish the opinion when it's in writing.

What it does not do

ChainMore Mini-MVP v1.0 is intentionally focused. It does not include:

  • Fiat off-ramp (USDC to EUR / USD bank transfer). That requires a licensed settlement partner; we integrate with one in v1.1+.
  • Cross-asset payments (e.g. customer pays USDT, merchant receives USDC). Token normalisation requires regulatory work we've explicitly deferred to v1.1. See our docs for the rationale.
  • Recurring billing or subscriptions. v2.0 surface.
  • Card payments. We are a stablecoin-only product; for card payments use a card processor.

Supported chains and assets | v1.0

  • Asset: USDC (native, Circle-issued). Not USDT, not bridged USDC.e.
  • Source chains: Ethereum, Base, Arbitrum, Polygon. Customers can pay from any of these.
  • Destination chains: Same set. Merchants can receive on any of these.
  • Cross-chain only. Same-chain USDC transfers go through our direct-payment path (also non-custodial), not the CCTP path described here.

Trust model and audits

We trust:

  • Circle: to issue USDC, run the CCTP attestor service, and not collude with attackers. Circle is a US-regulated stablecoin issuer.
  • The underlying chains: Ethereum, Base, Arbitrum, Polygon, for consensus and finality. We require N confirmations per chain to insulate from short-range reorgs.
  • The customer's wallet: to correctly sign the user's intent. The customer self-custodies; if their wallet is compromised, the funds at risk are their own.

We do not trust:

  • The customer (could be malicious; sanctions screening runs at intent creation).
  • The merchant (could be malicious; sanctions screening runs at intent creation).
  • Our own backend (single-key compromise of the gas-only wallet cannot redirect funds because the destination is bound at burn time).
  • Our own smart contract (third-party audit before mainnet, plus an Immunefi bug bounty).
